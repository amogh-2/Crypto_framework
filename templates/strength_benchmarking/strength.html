Benchmarking Based on Strength

Strength Parameters

This Page benchmarks the strength of various symmetric encryption algorithms, including AES (Advanced Encryption Standard) and ChaCha variants. The performance parameters evaluated here are critical in assessing cryptographic strength:

  • Avalanche Effect: Measures how much the ciphertext changes when a single bit in the plaintext or key is altered. A higher percentage (ideally 50%) indicates better diffusion and resistance to differential attacks.
  • Ciphertext Entropy: Quantifies the randomness of the encrypted output. Values close to 8 bits per byte (maximum entropy for 8-bit data) suggest the ciphertext is highly unpredictable and secure against statistical analysis.
  • Correlation Coefficient: Indicates the relationship between plaintext and ciphertext. A value near 0 implies minimal correlation, enhancing security by making it harder to deduce the plaintext from the ciphertext.
  • Key Size: Represents the length of the encryption key in bits. Larger keys (e.g., 256 bits) generally provide greater security against brute-force attacks due to the increased keyspace.

The algorithms compared include AES in CBC (Cipher Block Chaining) and GCM (Galois/Counter Mode) modes with 128-bit and 256-bit keys, as well as ChaCha20 and ChaCha-Poly (with Poly1305 authentication). These are widely used in modern cryptography, balancing security and performance.
